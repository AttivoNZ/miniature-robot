Welcome

Here is the honest version of why this newsletter exists.

I have spent the last several months pushing AI tools harder than most. Not experimenting — actually deploying. Building things. Breaking things. Charging clients for the output. And somewhere in the middle of all that, I realised two things simultaneously: this technology is more genuinely transformative than anything I have seen in 20 years of business, and almost everything being written about it is useless.

The breathless launches. The "10 prompts that will change your life." The LinkedIn posts where everything worked perfectly the first time. None of it maps to what actually happens when you try to use this stuff inside a real business with real constraints and a client on the other end who just wants the job done.

So this is the other thing. The week-behind version. We run a week late on purpose — not because we are slow, but because the dust needs to settle before you can see what actually happened. Breaking news in AI is mostly noise. What matters is what sticks.

Every issue has the same structure. What I am working on. What went wrong (and it always goes wrong). And the AI news that actually matters, stripped of the vendor spin.

We are still loading. So is everyone else. Let us figure it out together.

Regan

The Mistake

I had a workflow problem. A client needed a content and reporting system — something that could pull information together, organise it, surface the right things at the right time. Straightforward brief.

My first instinct was to be clever. Connect the tools. Multiple platforms stitched together with webhooks and API calls and conditional logic that looked very impressive in diagram form. I had built systems like this before. I knew how the pieces fit.

Three days in, I had something that technically worked. It also required a manual to operate, broke when any single connector had a hiccup, and needed me personally to maintain it every time something upstream changed. Classic over-engineering. I had built a machine to do the job of a tool that already existed.

The fix was embarrassingly simple. I threw out the whole stack and rebuilt natively — a single AI-driven environment, no connectors, no middleware, just the model and the interface. It took four hours. It was more reliable, faster to use, and the client understood it immediately without explanation.

The cleverest solution is almost never the right one. Connecting five tools when one will do it is not sophistication. It is insecurity dressed up as architecture.

I built the complicated version because it felt like proper work. The simple version felt too easy to charge for. That is the trap. And I walked straight into it.

The rule I have taken from it: if your workflow diagram has more than three boxes, start again.

AI News Worth Your Time — Week ending 9 March 2026
The jobs question is now unavoidable

The Morgan Stanley TMT conference in San Francisco had the biggest names in tech on stage, and the question that kept surfacing had nothing to do with models or compute. It was "what will our kids do?" Sam Altman told the room he can envision one to five people running an entire company, and said that transition has compressed to the next few years. That is not a prediction anymore. It is a timeline.

GPT-5.4 crossed a line quietly

OpenAI released a model that can autonomously execute multi-step workflows across software environments. On a benchmark simulating real desktop productivity tasks, it scored 75%. The human baseline is 72.4%. Note what that means: above human performance on economically valuable tasks. Not a party trick. The ballgame changing.

Anthropic drew a line and it cost them

Anthropic refused to allow its tools to be used for mass surveillance or autonomous weapons without human oversight. The Pentagon declared them a supply-chain risk — a designation normally reserved for foreign adversaries. Trump directed federal agencies to phase out Anthropic tools. OpenAI stepped in to fill the gap. You can have a view on who is right. But notice what it tells you about where this technology is heading and who wants to control it.

The cost floor keeps dropping

Model running costs continue to fall. Tools previously limited to large companies are now accessible to small businesses and independent operators. The moat is not access anymore. It is knowing what to do with it.
